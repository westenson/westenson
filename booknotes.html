Slaughterhouse-Five - Kurt Vonnegut

How Strongly I Recommend: 8/10

Massacre is unjustifiable. Free will is an illusion. Death will come. "So it goes".

My Notes:

Setting during and after WWI from 1940s to 1960s

Main character is Billy Pilgrim, odd dude who was always out of place growing up. Does well in school and enrolls in the Ilium School of Optometry. Drafted into army during WWII. His job is to be a chaplain's assistant.

Billy is captured by the Germans and put in a POW camp. At this point, he has his first incident of "time-shifting". He sees his entire life. The camp that Billy joins is filled with English officers who were captured earlier in the war. They have developed a relationship of mutual respect with their German guards. Thus, their POW camp is quite posh.

All while the story of Billy's war experience is told, we get moments of "time-shifting" where Billy gets catapulted to different points in his life.

After some days at the camp, Billy and the other Americans travel on to the city of Dresden, untouched from the war. Here they begin forced labor in a nutritional malt syrup factory, formerly a slaughterhouse for pigs.

One night, the city is bombarded by the Allied Forces, killing roughly 130,000 people. Bill and the Americans survive in the bunker of the slaughterhouse. Several days later the war ends and Billy heads back home.

Billy finishes optometry school and gets engaged to the daughter of the Dean. However, Billy has a breakdown and goes to the veterans hospital where he meets a fellow patient who introduces him to the science fiction author Kilgore Trout. After Billy leaves the hospital, he gets married and his father-in-law sets him up with his own optometry practice.

Years later, Billy explains to his daughter that he was kidnapped by aliens called Tralfamadorians. They took him to their planet, Tralfamadore, where they keep him in a zoo and have him mate with an abducted movie actress, Montana Wildhack.

“If I hadn’t spent so much time studying Earthlings,” said the Tralfamadorian, “I wouldn’t have any idea what was meant by ‘free will.’ I’ve visited thirty-one inhabited planets in the universe, and I have studied reports on one hundred more. Only on Earth is there any talk of free will.”

The Tralfamdorians explain that the see the universe in 4 dimensions, including time. They see the past, present, and future as one. Every part of a persons life is just an event at a particular time, so death is not so important; somewhere else, they are alive and well.

Back on earth, Billy gets on a chartered plain for an optometry conference. He knows that the plane will crash, but says nothing. After the crash, Billy undergoes brain surgery. His wife dies in a car accident on the way to see him. His daughter thinks he's crazy. Billy predicts his death with a tape recording and adds that he will only skip back to some other point in his life upon his killing.

At the beginning of the book, Vonnegut writes that the novel will end with the phrase "Poo-tee-weet." At the end of the war, the world is quiet, as it always is after a massacre. That is, except for birds. "Poo-tee-weet" is the only thing that is said after massacre, because no one can justify them. This is an antiwar book, clearly it shows here.

Throughout the book, the phrase "so it goes" appears after every mention of death. Billy learns this from the Tralfamadorians with the idea that a person is only dead in one particular moment. I imagine Vonnegut is symbolizing the desensitization of death during war.

Overarching themes: Vonnegut inserts himself in the story throughout the book, showing us that he too was a POW in Dresden. His main theme is the destructiveness of war and the lack of justification for so many deaths. The story of Billy Pilgrim shows how life is disrupted long after the war is over. The Tralfmadorian theory of the 4th dimension is the only way Billy can justify the destructiveness of war, an idea that is clearly far from reality.

There is also some overarching theme of free will. The Tralfamdorians theory of the 4th dimension make it obvious. all moments of life are occurring simultaneously so everything in life is determined. Billy is a weak solider without a gun, yet he survives and many great soldiers do not. Billy's effort do not carve his path in life, it is made for him.

Death will come. "So it goes".

Innovators - by Walter Isaacson

How Strongly I Recommend: 10/10

History of the computer and the internet by one of the best biographers of all time IMO

My Notes:

Ada Lovelace - gifted at mathematics and considered the first computer programmer. Worked with Charles Babbage on Babbage's Difference Engine, a mechanical machine that could calculate polynomial functions. Lovelace later proposed the Analytical Engine, an improved version of the Difference Engine. Died in 1852.

AT&T's Bell Labs was the "idea factory" during and after WWII where hundreds of scientists and engineers were given a relatively free hand to explore and improvise. Important names here are John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley; they created the transistor, the fundamental building block of the microprocessor.

Not long after, Gordon Moore predicted the doubling of a microprocessor's power every year and a half, now known as Moore's Law which has been incredibly accurate

Universities and the U.S. military created the network ARPANET in 1969 which was opened to more companies in 1973.

Another innovation hotspot was Xerox PARC in the 1970s with innovations including ethernet, the graphic user interface and the mouse. A popular phrase at the park was "the best way to predict the future is to invent it."

Bill Gates was a computer maniac, lucky to have grown up with access to one. Microsoft was founded with the foresight that hardware would soon become commodified (with low profits) and software would rule the world. We know how that bet turned out.

The beginnings of the personal computer can be traced back to the Homebrew Computer Club. Who was in the club? None other than Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak. Jobs was a visionary with an acute sense for aesthetic and user interaction.

Overarching themes: The internet and computer were built through an incremental and continuous effort of people from different backgrounds and different skills. Although "open-source" was not a term at the time, this is how the internet and computer were created and we wouldn't have it today if it wasn't for people's willingness to share their discoveries for all to benefit and push the industry forward.

This book is a series of mini biographies for a reason. It took many people to create the Digital Revolution. Isaacson attributes a powerful role in this revolution to ecosystems. Silicon Valley includes venture capital, strong universities churning out smart computer scientists and engineers and even significant government funding. All these came together to provide the perfect environment for innovation.

Outliers - by Malcolm Gladwell

How Strongly I Recommend: 10/10

Understanding success and values in the modern world. Must read.

My Notes:

Outliers are people who do not fit our conventional understanding of achievement

High IQ does not increase your chances of success by much. What matters the most is practical intelligence (ie. knowing what to say to whom, when, and how). This became clear through a research study does by Professor Lewis Madison Terman who conducted a longitudinal study of 1,500 with IQs of 150+. There was almost no correlation between success and IQ found in the experiment. "Anything above an IQ of 120 points has no significant impact on the attainment of success".

"Success is a function of persistence and doggedness and the willingness to work hard for 22 minutes when most people would give up after 30 seconds".

Success has a degree of opportunity and is not solely based on hard work. Success relies on opportunities, circumstances, and hidden advantages as well. It makes a difference where and when you grow up.

When you are deliberate about tapping into opportunities (creating and pursing), you have a much higher chance of mastery and success

With commitment, anyone can become an outlier. Think 10,000 hours here. Something that all successful people examined had was an intense commitment to their craft. Deep work with feedback required.

List America's richest people in history, 14/75 were born in the 1860s and '70s. This was the industrial revolution taking off. The same thing happened in Silicon Valley; all the top early tech entrepreneurs were born between 1953 and 1956. Those born in 1935, there were 600,000 fewer babies born that year which meant smaller class sizes, better chance to get on good sports teams and colleges, and, thus, better chances of getting great jobs.

Works the other way as well. If you were born in the 1890s to early 1900, these people face the Spanish Flu, WWI, the Great Depression, and were still young enough to be drafted in WWII (if they hadn't been killed already!).

Our values are unconsciously handed down to us from generation to generation. When analyzing different country's cultural tendencies, we find stark differences. Psychologist, Geert Hofstede, identified several different dimensions. These include Individualism vs Collectivism (ie. how much we expect you to look after yourself vs the group), Uncertainty Avoidance (ie how well one tolerates ambiguity), and Power-Distance Index (ie attitudes towards hierarchy).

Overarching themes: When opportunity presents itself, seize it. Its often quite arbitrary (ie. birthdates, right place right time), but you can increase your chances of it arising.

Timing is critical to success and opporuntity

There is no short cut to mastery than putting in the hours, 10,000 if you want a number. (meaningful, deliberate work is required)
